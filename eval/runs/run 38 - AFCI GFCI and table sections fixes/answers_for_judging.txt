=== cec2022-016 ===
Q: What is the difference between a main bonding jumper and a system bonding jumper? When is each required?

A: ## Main Bonding Jumper vs. System Bonding Jumper (CEC 2022)

### **Definitions**
- **Main Bonding Jumper** (CEC 2022 Section 100):  
  *The connection between the grounded circuit conductor and the equipment grounding conductor at the **service**.*  
  - **Required at services** (250.28).

- **System Bonding Jumper** (CEC 2022 Section 100):  
  *The connection between the grounded circuit conductor and the supply-side bonding jumper or equipment grounding conductor at a **separately derived system**.*  
  - **Required at separately derived systems** (250.28).

---

### **Key Differences**
| Feature | **Main Bonding Jumper** | **System Bonding Jumper** |
|---------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Location** | At the **service** (e.g., utility meter disconnect). | At **separately derived systems** (e.g., transformers, generators, solar inverters). |
| **Purpose** | Bonds grounded conductor to equipment grounding conductor **at the service**. | Bonds grounded conductor to equipment grounding conductor **at a separately derived system**. |
| **Applicable Code** | 250.28(D) for sizing. | 250.28(D) for sizing. |
| **Sizing Table** | Table 250.102(C)(1), based on largest ungrounded service conductor. | Table 250.102(C)(1), based on largest ungrounded conductor in the separately derived system. |

---

### **When Each is Required**
1. **Main Bonding Jumper**  
   - **Required at every service** (250.28).  
   - Ensures the grounded (neutral) conductor is bonded to the equipment grounding conductor **at the service point**.  
   - Example: A residential service panel.

2. **System Bonding Jumper**  
   - **Required at every separately derived system** (250.28).  
   - Ensures the grounded conductor is bonded to the equipment grounding conductor **at the source of the separately derived system**.  
   - Example: A 480V to 208V transformer or a solar PV inverter.

---

### **Sizing Both Jumpers**
Both are sized using **Table 250.102(C)(1)** based on the largest ungrounded conductor in their respective systems.  
**Example**:  
- For a service with 4/0 AWG copper service conductors:  
  - Main bonding jumper = **4 AWG copper** (Table 250.102(C)(1)).  
- For a separately derived system with 350 kcmil aluminum feeders:  
  - System bonding jumper = **2/0 AWG copper** (Table 250.102(C)(1)).

---

### **Inspector Note**
- **Main bonding jumper** is part of the service grounding system.  
- **System bonding jumper** is part of the separately derived system’s grounding.  
- Both must be **copper, aluminum, or corrosion-resistant material** (250.28(A)).  
- **Never substitute** a system bonding jumper for a main bonding jumper, or vice versa.

Q: Why is AFCI protection required for bedrooms and living areas? What electrical hazard does it prevent?

A: ## AFCI Protection in Bedrooms and Living Areas (CEC 2022)

**Requirement**:  
Arc-fault circuit-interrupter (AFCI) protection is required for **15- and 20-ampere, 120-volt branch circuits** supplying outlets in dwelling unit bedrooms, living rooms, and similar areas (Section 210.12(A)). This includes family rooms, dining rooms, dens, sunrooms, recreation rooms, and hallways.

**Electrical Hazard Prevented**:  
AFCI protection prevents **electrical fires caused by arc faults**. Arc faults occur when damaged or degraded wiring creates high-resistance connections, generating heat that can ignite surrounding materials. Common causes include:  
- Damaged cords or appliances  
- Loose or corroded connections  
- Rodent-chewed wiring  
- Overloaded circuits  

**Key Details**:  
- **Arc Fault Mechanism**: Arcs can reach temperatures exceeding 10,000°F (5,500°C), easily igniting wood, insulation, or other flammable materials.  
- **AFCI Function**: Detects unique electrical signatures of dangerous arcs (e.g., series arcs in damaged wiring) and interrupts the circuit before a fire starts.  
- **Scope**: Applies to **outlet branch circuits** (not feeders) in areas where arcing is more likely due to frequent use of portable devices and higher wiring density.  

**Code Basis**:  
- **CEC 2022 Section 210.12(A)** explicitly mandates AFCI protection for these areas.  
- **Informational Note**: References UL 1699 standards and NFPA 72 for fire alarm systems, emphasizing fire prevention.  

**Inspector Note**:  
AFCI requirements **do not apply to GFCI-protected circuits** (e.g., bathrooms, kitchens) or dedicated circuits for fire alarm systems. Always verify the first outlet location and marking per 210.12(A)(2)–(6) for compliance.

Q: Why are torque specifications important when terminating conductors in a panel, and where are the requirements found?

A: ## Torque Specifications for Conductor Terminations (CEC 2022)

Torque specifications are critical for ensuring **secure, low-resistance electrical connections** in panel terminations. Improper torque can lead to:
- **Under-torqued connections**: Loose terminations causing arcing, overheating, and potential fire hazards.
- **Over-torqued connections**: Damaged conductors or terminals, leading to mechanical failure or compromised conductivity.

### Requirements Location
The California Electrical Code (CEC 2022) addresses torque specifications in **Section 110.14(D)**:

#### **CEC 2022 Section 110.14(D) - Terminal Connection Torque**
> "Tightening torque values for terminal connections shall be as indicated on equipment or in installation instructions provided by the manufacturer. An approved means shall be used to achieve the indicated torque value."

**Key Requirements:**
1. **Manufacturer Specifications**: Torque values must match those marked on the equipment or in the manufacturer's installation instructions.
2. **Approved Tools**: Use torque tools, shear bolts, or breakaway devices to verify proper torque application.

2. **Neutral Bar Bonded to Metal Enclosure**  
   - **Violation**: CEC 250.142(A) prohibits bonding the neutral bar to the subpanel enclosure. This creates an unintended grounding path and violates the requirement for neutral isolation in subpanels.  
   - **Correct Configuration**: The neutral bar must remain **unbonded** from the enclosure. Only the **equipment grounding conductor (EGC)** connects the enclosure to the ground bar.

   - **Violation**: CEC 250.32(B) mandates a **separate grounding electrode** (e.g., ground rod) for subpanels in detached structures. The subpanel relies solely on the feeder ground wire, which is insufficient.

   - **Feeder ground**: Connected to the **ground bar** (bonded to the enclosure via EGC).  

2. **Grounding Electrode**  
   - A **separate ground rod** (or other electrode) is installed at the detached garage.  
   - The **GEC** connects the ground rod to the subpanel’s ground bar (not the neutral bar).

California's medium voltage (MV) cable requirements are detailed in **CEC 2022 Sections 311.60 and 392.80**, with specific ampacity tables for Type MV cables (rated 2001–35,000 volts). Below are the key tables and their applications:

---

### **1. Ampacity Tables for Type MV Cables**
#### **Multiconductor Cables**
- **Table 311.60(C)(75)** and **Table 311.60(C)(76)**  
  - **Application**: Multiconductor Type MV cables in cable trays.  
  - **Adjustments**:  
    - If trays are **continuously covered >6 ft** with solid unventilated covers, use **95% of listed ampacities**.  
    - For **uncovered trays** with spacing ≥1 cable diameter, use **Table 311.60(C)(71)** and **Table 311.60(C)(72)**.  

#### **Single-Conductor Cables**
- **Table 311.60(C)(69)** and **Table 311.60(C)(70)**  
  - **Application**: Single-conductor Type MV cables in cable trays.  
  - **Adjustments**:  
    - For **covered trays >6 ft**, use **70% of listed ampacities** (1/0 AWG+).  
    - For **uncovered trays** with spacing ≥1 cable diameter, use **Table 311.60(C)(67)** and **Table 311.60(C)(68)**.  

---

### **2. Installation-Specific Rules**
- **Section 392.80(B)** governs ampacity adjustments based on cable tray installation:  
  - **Covered trays**: Apply derating factors (e.g., 70–75% of table values).  
  - **Spacing requirements**: Maintain ≥1 cable diameter spacing between conductors.  
  - **Triplex/quadruplex configurations**: Use **Table 311.60(C)(67)**–**72** for triangular/square arrangements.  

---

### **3. Key Definitions**
- **Type MV Cable**: Solid dielectric-insulated cable rated 2001–35,000 volts (Section 311.2).  
- **Engineering Supervision**: Permitted for non-table-based ampacity calculations (Section 311.60(A)).  

---

### **4. California-Specific Notes**
- **CALGreen Compliance**: Ensure grounding and bonding meet Title 24 Part 10 for MV systems.  
- **Termination Limits**: Verify conductor temperature ratings per **Section 110.14(C)**.

Q: What is the minimum working space depth for a 480V panelboard under Condition 3?

A: ## Dedicated Working Space Requirements (CEC 2022 110.26(B))

**CEC 2022 Section 110.26(B)** requires that the working space around electrical equipment be **dedicated** and **not used for storage** or other purposes. While the search tool incorrectly mapped this to Section 646.18 (a general reference), the actual requirement is:

> **110.26(B) Dedicated Space**  
> The working space required by 110.26(A) shall be **dedicated** to the electrical equipment and **not used for storage** or other purposes. This space must remain **clear at all times** to ensure safe access for operation, maintenance, and inspection.

---

### Key Implications:
1. **No Storage Allowed:**  
   - The 4 ft (1.2 m) working space depth **must remain unobstructed** by tools, boxes, or other items.
   - Violates 110.26(B) if storage is placed in this area.

2. **Permanently Clear Access:**  
   - The space must be **maintained** to allow unrestricted movement for servicing equipment.

3. **Complementary to 240.24(D):**  
   - While 240.24(D) prohibits panelboards in clothes closets, **110.26(B)** ensures the working space itself is not compromised by storage—even in permitted locations.

---

### Inspector Note:  
Always verify both **dimensional compliance** (Table 110.26(A)(1)) and **dedicated space compliance** (110.26(B)) during inspections. A panel may meet clearance depth requirements but still fail if the space is used for storage.

   7 conductors → **0.70** (Table 310.15(C)(1))

   $$
   50A \times 0.88 \times 0.70 = 30.8A
   $$
